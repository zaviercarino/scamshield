Family Emergency Scams

Family emergency scams exploit fear by convincing victims that a loved one is in danger and urgently needs money or personal information.

Scammers may pretend to be a family member, friend, police officer, lawyer, or hospital employee. They often claim someone has been arrested, injured, kidnapped, or stranded and needs immediate financial help.

How Family Emergency Scams Work

📞

The Fake Emergency

The scammer contacts you claiming a loved one is facing a serious emergency that requires immediate action.

😨

The Pressure

They create panic by insisting there's no time to verify the story and may ask you to keep the situation secret.

💸

The Payment

You're instructed to send money through gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or payment apps that are difficult to reverse.

The Catch

Scammers rely on fear and urgency so you act emotionally instead of verifying whether the emergency is real.

The Scam

Once you send money or share sensitive information, the scammer disappears and the emergency never existed.

Protect Yourself

Learn how to verify emergency claims before sending money or sharing personal information.

Warning Signs / Red Flags
  • An unexpected call or message claiming a loved one is in trouble.
  • Pressure to act immediately without verifying the situation.
  • Requests to keep the emergency secret from other family members.
  • Demands for payment through gift cards, wire transfers, cryptocurrency, or payment apps.
  • The caller refuses to let you speak directly with your loved one.
  • The story contains vague or inconsistent details.
  • The caller claims traditional payment methods won't work.
  • Threats that delaying payment will make the situation worse.
  • The caller claims to be a lawyer, police officer, or hospital employee but cannot verify their identity.
  • The message creates panic and discourages questions.
How to Stay Safe
  • Stay calm and don't rush into sending money.
  • Contact your family member directly using a trusted phone number.
  • Verify the story with another relative or friend.
  • Never rely solely on caller ID, which can be spoofed.
  • Ask questions only your loved one would know.
  • Be suspicious of requests for secrecy.
  • Never send money before independently confirming the emergency.
  • Consider creating a family safe word for real emergencies.
  • Be cautious of AI-generated voices that imitate family members.
  • Report suspicious calls or messages.
What To Do If You've Been Scammed
  • Contact your bank or payment provider immediately.
  • Report the incident to local law enforcement.
  • Warn your family members about the scam.
  • Save messages, phone numbers, receipts, and payment records.
  • Report the scam to the platform or service used.
  • Monitor your financial accounts for suspicious activity.
  • Change passwords if personal information was shared.
  • Be cautious of anyone claiming they can recover your money for a fee.
Quick Checklist
  • Did I verify the emergency independently?
  • Can I speak directly with my family member?
  • Am I being pressured to act immediately?
  • Am I being asked to keep this a secret?
  • Am I being asked to use an unusual payment method?
  • Does the story make logical sense?
  • Have I spoken with another family member?
  • Could this be someone exploiting my emotions?
