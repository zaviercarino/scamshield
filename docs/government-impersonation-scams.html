Government Impersonation Scams

Government impersonation scams occur when criminals pretend to be government agencies or officials to pressure victims into sending money or sharing personal information.

Scammers commonly impersonate the IRS, Social Security Administration, DMV, law enforcement, immigration officials, or local government agencies. They often claim you owe money, missed jury duty, or face legal consequences unless you act immediately.

How Government Impersonation Scams Work

📞

The Fake Official

The scammer contacts you by phone, text, email, or mail pretending to represent a government agency.

⚠️

The Threat

They claim you owe taxes, have unpaid fines, or face arrest, account suspension, or legal action unless you respond immediately.

💵

The Payment

Victims are pressured to pay using gift cards, cryptocurrency, wire transfers, or other difficult-to-recover payment methods.

The Catch

Scammers rely on fear, authority, and urgency to stop victims from thinking critically or verifying the request.

The Scam

Once victims send money or provide sensitive information, recovering either is often extremely difficult.

Protect Yourself

Learn how legitimate government agencies actually communicate and recognize the warning signs of impersonation scams.

Warning Signs / Red Flags
  • You receive an unexpected call, email, or text claiming to be from a government agency.
  • You are threatened with arrest, deportation, lawsuits, or fines.
  • The message demands immediate payment.
  • You are told not to tell anyone about the situation.
  • You are instructed to pay with gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers.
  • The caller refuses to let you verify their identity.
  • The message creates intense urgency or fear.
  • You are asked for sensitive information such as your Social Security number or banking details.
  • The phone number or email address looks suspicious.
  • The scammer claims immediate action is required to avoid legal consequences.
How to Stay Safe
  • Hang up and contact the agency using its official website or phone number.
  • Never pay government agencies using gift cards or cryptocurrency.
  • Do not share personal information unless you initiated the contact.
  • Be skeptical of threats demanding immediate action.
  • Verify unexpected notices independently.
  • Ignore caller ID since it can be spoofed.
  • Keep records of suspicious communications.
  • Discuss suspicious messages with someone you trust.
  • Report impersonation attempts to the appropriate authorities.
  • Remember that legitimate agencies generally allow time to resolve issues.
What To Do If You've Been Scammed
  • Stop communicating with the scammer immediately.
  • Contact your bank or payment provider if you sent money.
  • Report the scam to the impersonated government agency.
  • Monitor your financial accounts for suspicious activity.
  • Consider placing a fraud alert if personal information was shared.
  • Save messages, emails, and phone numbers as evidence.
  • Change passwords if account information was compromised.
  • Report the scam to local consumer protection authorities.
Quick Checklist
  • Did I expect to hear from this government agency?
  • Am I being threatened with immediate consequences?
  • Am I being pressured to pay right now?
  • Am I being asked to use gift cards or cryptocurrency?
  • Have I verified this request using an official government website or phone number?
  • Is the caller refusing to let me verify their identity?
  • Does the message create fear or panic?
  • Would a legitimate government agency normally handle things this way?
